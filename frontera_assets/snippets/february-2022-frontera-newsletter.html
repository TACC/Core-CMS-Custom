Identifying Individual Proteins Using Nanopores and Supercomputers

Researchers from the University of Illinois published a proof-of-concept study in Science showing it is possible to identify individual proteins with single-amino acid resolution. Their method uses nanopores and relied on powerful supercomputers, including Frontera at TACC, Blue Waters at NCSA, and Expanse at SDSC.

Thriving in Non-Equilibrium

Frontera enabled researchers from the University of Alabama at Birmingham and Clemson University to explore light-induced superconductivity using pulsed lasers. Writing in Physical Review X, they showed that d-wave superconductivity can be enhanced by a pulsed laser, but the light-enhanced superconductivity may be of a fluctuating nature.

When Water is Coming from All Sides

Since April 2021, researchers from NOAA and William & Mary have run daily 2D and 3D forecasts on Frontera of the first 3D compound inland-coastal flooding guidance system. During Tropical Storm Claudette (June 2021), water level results from the three-dimensional, real-time model closely matched observations across the Gulf Coast.

Artificial intelligence-enhanced quantum chemical method with broad applicability

Zheng, P., Zubatyuk, R., Wu, W. et al. Nat Commun 12, 7022 (2021). DOI: doi.org/10.1038/s41467-021-27340-2

High-level quantum mechanical (QM) calculations are indispensable for explaining natural phenomena on the atomistic level. Their computational cost, however, poses great limitations, which luckily can be reduced by exploiting advances in artificial intelligence (AI). The researchers introduce a general-purpose, highly transferable artificial intelligenceâ€“quantum mechanical method 1 (AIQM1). It approaches the accuracy of the gold-standard coupled cluster QM method with high computational speed.

FEATURED VIDEO

Visualization of an Above Anvil Cirrus Plume

Leigh Orf, University of Wisconsin; Greg Foss, Dave Semeraro, TACC

Atmospheric scientists used Frontera to simulate a thunderstorm anvil, a weather feature that frequently accompanies tornadoes. Understanding this phenomenon could improve severe weather forecasts. In collaboration with TACC visualization experts, the researchers presented their research (which was featured on the cover of Science magazine in September 2021) as part of the SC21 Visualization Showcase.
