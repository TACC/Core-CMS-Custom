Simulating 800,000 years of California Earthquake History to Pinpoint Risks

Seismologists used Frontera to simulate hundreds of thousands of years of seismic history in California using a new framework. The research pinpoints how large an earthquake might occur at a given location, allowing building code developers and engineers to design structures that can survive earthquakes.

Evolving the Early Universe in 24 Hours on Frontera

During the December 2020 Texascale Days event, researchers made progress on the "Asterix" simulation: the largest cosmological simulation ever performed at this resolution. The simulation peaked at 7,911 nodes (or more than 400,000 processors) in a single job and included 5,500^3 dark matter and gas particles and almost 400 billion elements.

Powering COVID-19 Collaborations and Communicating Risk to Public

The epidemiological models developed by the UT Austin COVID-19 Modeling Consortium compute daily forecasts on Frontera. The COVID-19 Portal that hosts the model reflects state-of-the-art parallel computing, software engineering, data visualization, and portal design, enabled in part by staff at TACC.

Protein Storytelling to Address the Pandemic

Stony Brook University researcher Ken Dill is using TACC’s Frontera supercomputer to make structure predictions for 19 proteins from the SARS-CoV-2 virus. Each of these proteins could serve as an avenue for new drug developments.

Gordon Bell ‘Special Prize’ Winning Team Reveals AI Workflow for Molecular Systems in the Era of COVID-19

A team led by Rommie Amaro (University of California, San Diego) and Arvind Ramanathan (Argonne National Laboratory) used Frontera to explore how the COVID-19 spike protein gains access to the human cell. The work earned the team the Gordon Bell ‘Special Prize’ for High Performance Computing-Based COVID-19 Research.

Still from an animation of the inspiral of a binary black hole with a 128:1 mass ratio showing the beginning of the final burst of gravitational waves. [Credit: Carlos Lousto, James Healy, RIT.]

Final Dance of Unequal Black Hole Partners

In 2005, Carlos Lousto was among the first researchers to simulate the impacts of two black holes merging. A decade later, his results were observationally confirmed by LIGO. Recently, Lousto and James Healy, both of Rochester Institute of Technology, used TACC’s Frontera supercomputer to model for the first time a merger of two black holes with very different sizes, a ratio of 128 to 1.

Call for Leadership-Class Computing Facility Application Partners

NSF issued a ‘Dear Colleague Letter’ for science teams with current or future requirements exceeding those available on today’s large-scale scientific computers. NSF will award up to $150,000 per year to research teams to work with TACC to improve codes and help drive requirements for the design of the NSF Leadership-Class Computing Facility.

Advancing Characteristic Science Applications

Frontera, TACC's NSF-funded leadership-class advanced computing system, will receive an expansion to support urgent computing and basic science for the national research community.

Frontera Expansion to Support Urgent Computing

Frontera added 11 compute racks thanks to a supplemental award from NSF and contributions from Dell Technologies and Intel. The new hardware will support urgent computing efforts, including COVID-19 tracking and modeling and hurricane forecasting. The award from NSF also allows the academic community to double its usage of Longhorn, a subsystem of Frontera that supports deep learning workloads.
