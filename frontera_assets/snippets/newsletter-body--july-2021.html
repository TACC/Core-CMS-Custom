Happy 20th Anniversary, TACC!

On June 1, 2001, the center began its journey to becoming one of the leading academic supercomputing centers in the world. We look forward to formally celebrating with an event later this year. In the meantime, we present highlights and accomplishments from the past two decades. Stay tuned for more details!

Visualization of the wind field of Hurricane Laura (2020) and the percentage of a building's repair cost to its replacement value in Lake Charles, Louisiana. [Credit: NHERI SimCenter]

ENVISIONING SAFER CITIES WITH AI

SimCenter researchers developed a suite of AI tools that automatically identify characteristics of buildings and simulate the risks structures face during a natural hazard event. The team used Frontera to train the model and run testbed simulations for earthquakes in San Francisco and hurricanes in Lake Charles, Louisiana, along the Texas coast, and in Atlantic City, New Jersey.

A multiscale model of the complete SARS-CoV-2 virion has been developed for the first time using supercomputers. [Credit: Gregory Voth, University of Chicago]

FIRST COMPLETE CORONAVIRUS MODEL SHOWS COOPERATION

The first-ever model of the complete SARS-CoV-2 virion showed the cooperative motion of the coronavirus spike protein, which is likely how the virus explores and detects the ACE2 receptors of a potential host cell. Frontera generated all-atom simulations of the coronavirus spike protein system that contributed to the larger coarse-grained model.

July 12, 2012 coronal mass ejection seen in coronagraphs, and, below, overlapped with model results. [Credit: Talwinder Singh, Mehmet S. Yalim, Nikolai V. Pogorelov, and Nat Gopalswamy]

WHICH WAY DOES THE SOLAR WIND BLOW?

The bipartisan PROSWIFT Act, passed into law in October 2020, formalized the need to develop better space weather forecasts. High performance computers are central to such efforts. With funding from NSF and NASA, University of Alabama scientists are using Frontera to improve the state-of-the-art in space weather forecasting software.

Red blood cell hitchhiking enhances the accumulation of nano- and micro-particles in the constriction of a stenosed microvessel. [Credit: Sarah Shattuck and Dr. Huilin Ye]

TARGETING TUMORS WITH NANOWORMS

Engineers modeled the movement of nanomedicines through the human vascular system and found that nanoworms — thin, engineered encapsulations of drug contents — can travel through red blood cell traffic better than spherical nanoparticles. The team is using the results of their simulations on Frontera to train a machine learning model to design patient-specific treatments.

A plot showing how the decay properties of a meson made from a heavy quark and a light quark change when the lattice spacing and heavy quark mass are varied on the calculation. [Credit: A. Bazavov et al.]

SEARCHING FOR HINTS OF NEW PHYSICS IN THE SUBATOMIC WORLD

Lattice QCD uses supercomputers to study the interactions between quarks and gluons, the subatomic particles that make up ordinary matter. Two leading contemporary scholars of lattice QCD are using Frontera to explore the ‘anomalous magnetic moment of the muon,’ a hot topic in the particle physics world.

The IceCube Neutrino Observatory is the first detector of its kind, designed to observe the cosmos from deep within the South Pole ice. [Credit: Yuya Makino, IceCube/NSF]

TRACKING COSMIC GHOSTS

In March 2021, the IceCube Neutrino Observatory announced the detection of high-energy electron anti-neutrinos, providing further validation of the Standard Model of particle physics. Frontera was among the resources used to detect and confirm the discovery.

The 58 projects receiving Large Resource Allocation awards on Frontera will utilize a total of 58.3 million node hours.

FRONTERA LRAC AWARDS ANNOUNCED

NSF approved allocations of supercomputing time on Frontera for 58 science projects for 2021-2022, led by researchers at 41 universities across 23 states. The projects range from efforts to engineer new nanomaterials to studies mapping pan-Arctic permafrost thaw.

GOOGLE CLOUD INTEGRATION WITH FRONTERA

Frontera's integration with cloud services provides scholars with new options for data storage and access to emerging computing technologies. Starting in May, Frontera researchers gained access to computational capabilities available through Google Cloud (such as Tensor processors), in addition to those already provided through Microsoft Azure and Amazon Web Services. Learn how to connect.

SMALL JOBS, SMALL WAIT

To improve the throughput of Frontera jobs, a ‘small’ queue has been created specifically for one and two node jobs. The normal queue now has a lower limit of three nodes for all jobs. The change will improve the turnaround time for jobs in both the normal and small queues, and will also increase the already high utilization rate.

FEATURED PUBLICATION

AI-assisted superresolution cosmological simulations

Yin Li, Yueying Ni, Rupert A. C. Croft, Tiziana Di Matteo, Simeon Bird, Yu Feng
Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS) May 2021, 118 (19) e2022038118; DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2022038118

Cosmological simulations are indispensable for understanding our Universe. Researchers built a deep neural network on Frontera to enhance low-resolution dark-matter simulations, generating superresolution realizations that agree with authentic high-resolution counterparts and are orders-of-magnitude faster.
